The Hidden Cost of Poor Air Quality: How Pollution Quietly Undermines Urban Economies and Health

As cities grapple with the growing burden of air pollution, its impact extends far beyond environmental concerns. This project delves into how degraded air quality covertly strains urban economies, from lost productivity to rising healthcare costs. By examining data on pollution levels, economic output, and health outcomes, we reveal the often-overlooked financial and societal costs of air pollution—highlighting the urgent need for informed, data-driven policy interventions.

Project Aims

Investigating the Relationship Between Air Pollution and Life Expectancy

  • Use machine learning to study how PM2.5 levels affect life expectancy in different regions.
  • Analyze the role of factors like healthcare spending, urbanization, and economic growth.

Comparing Regional Disparities

  • Highlight differences in air quality and health impacts across regions.
  • Investigate social and economic factors contributing to these variations.

Evaluating Environmental and Economic Trade-Offs

  • Study whether economic growth leads to better or worse air quality by examining the relationship between GDP per capita and PM2.5 levels.
  • Assess how policies and regional strategies influence air pollution levels.

Supporting Policymaking with Data

  • Create visualizations to show the links between air pollution, health, and economic outcomes.
  • Provide actionable recommendations to reduce pollution and improve health outcomes fairly.

Data Use and Access

I used several datasets to explore PM2.5 air pollution and its effects. PM2.5 concentration data came from the WHO Ambient Air Quality Database, a Wikipedia page, as well as NASA's SEDAC. Life expectancy data was sourced from Our World in Data and the WHO Global Health Observatory. Socioeconomic data, such as GDP per healthcare spending, was collected from World Bank. Data about the mortality rates were sourced from the Global Health Observatory's API

To ensure automation, I used Python’s BeautifulSoup library to scrape PM2.5 data from Wikipedia. API endpoints were used for WHO and other sources to make data retrieval reproducible. All scripts were stored on GitHub for transparency and replication. I cleaned and merged data using pandas, ensuring consistent formatting with ISO3 codes for country-level analysis.

This automated approach allowed consistent and replicable data handling. Any missing or incomplete rows were carefully filtered out to avoid errors. By combining web scraping, APIs, and manual data integration, I ensured the datasets were comprehensive for analysis. These steps formed the foundation for creating interactive visualizations and statistical models.

Challenges in Data Cleaning and Analysis

I faced several challenges while cleaning and analyzing the data. Integrating datasets from different sources was not straightforward. Many formats and identifiers, such as country names, were inconsistent. Some countries lacked ISO3 codes. I used Python's pandas library to standardize these formats. This made merging the datasets possible.

Missing data was another issue. Temporal datasets, like PM2.5 levels and life expectancy, had gaps or incomplete records. Missing values could have introduced bias. I filtered out incomplete rows and kept only data with enough observations.

Visualizing multi-variable relationships was also challenging. Overlapping data points or unclear trends could reduce the impact of the charts. I solved this using Vega-Lite to create interactive visualizations. Features like tooltips and sliders allowed users to explore the data more intuitively.

Automation played an important role. I used BeautifulSoup to scrape PM2.5 data from online sources. I structured my workflow in Jupyter Notebooks for clarity. This ensured that my process was well-documented and easy to replicate.

Conclusion

Findings showed significant regional disparities in air quality, with South Asia and the Middle East experiencing the highest PM2.5 levels. Oceania maintained consistently low pollution levels, highlighting the role of strong environmental policies and lower industrial activity. These results emphasize that regional strategies are critical in addressing air pollution.

The GDP per capita analysis revealed that economic growth does not always result in reduced air pollution. For instance, while North America had lower PM2.5 levels, Europe exhibited higher concentrations despite similar income levels, pointing to differences in industrial practices and policies. The correlation analysis between PM2.5 and Non-Communicable Disease (NCD) mortality (R²=0.35) indicated that air pollution contributes to health challenges like cardiovascular and respiratory diseases. Similarly, the relationship between PM2.5 and life expectancy (R²=0.40) underscored the severe impacts on regions like Bangladesh and India, where pollution exacerbates low life expectancy.

This analysis had limitations. Data gaps excluded key regions, potentially skewing findings. Temporal coverage (2010–2019) limited understanding of recent trends and interventions. Statistical models revealed correlations, not causation, and other factors like healthcare access or policies were not fully addressed.

Overall, this project provided actionable insights and highlighted the need for targeted, data-driven interventions to mitigate air pollution’s impacts.
